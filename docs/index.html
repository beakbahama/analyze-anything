The AAF is a protocol for a partnership between minds, helping human and synthetic reasoning work together more coherently. The method is a simple, three-stage dialogue.

  • 1. You Wield the Prompt.

  • 2. The Engine Audits Reality.

  • 3. You Forge the Insight.

The Gauntlet: A Three-Tiered Audit

The AAF runs any idea through a three-stage gauntlet, testing not for what is popular, but for what is real.

  • 1. Is It Valid? – First, it checks for basic sanity. Is the work a coherent signal, or is it just a "word salad"?

    In a business plan, this is the ruthless check for logistical reality. In a scientific theory, it's the hunt for a non-falsifiable claim. In a relationship, it's the audit for gaslighting—the moment you prove their words do not match the data of their actions.

  • 2. Is It Rigorous? – Next, it stress-tests the craftsmanship. Is the logic ironclad? Is it parsimonious? Or is it a beautiful but hollow structure?

    In a scientific theory, this is the search for ignored counter-evidence. In a work of art, it's the test for sloppy craftsmanship. In a relationship, it's the exposure of hypocrisy—the beautiful, soaring promises that are built on a foundation of inconsistent, hollow actions.

  • 3. Does It Matter? – Finally, it tests for significance. Does the work create a genuine paradigm shift, or is it just a clever iteration on an old idea?

    In science, this is the test for a true revolution in thought. In art, it is the birth of a new and influential form. In a relationship, it's the moment of brutal, terrifying honesty that shatters a comfortable, decades-long lie and makes a true, coherent partnership possible for the first time.

  • Bonus Audit: Audit the Audit – An additional final part of the framework turns inward. It cautions against using the first three tiers too bluntly. It reminds the analyst (human or synthetic) that every act of evaluation is itself grounded in unprovable axioms, perceptual limits, and interpretive filters.

    This part checks whether the work is being judged fairly—on its own terms, in its own medium, with awareness of its intended purpose. It asks whether the analysis has identified the claim’s core axioms—those unproven seeds of logic that everything else grows from.

    Most importantly, it enforces the Humility Clause: the possibility that the idea isn’t incoherent—it’s just beyond you. Some ideas are not false. They are simply pre-paradigmatic, or born from a logic the analyst has not yet grown into.
